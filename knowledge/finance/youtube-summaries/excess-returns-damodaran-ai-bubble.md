# The Bubble Most Will Get Wrong | Aswath Damodaran on How He Is Investing in a World of AI

**Channel:** Excess Returns | **Words:** 12040

## One-Sentence Summary
Professor Aswath Damodaran explains AI bubble dynamics infrastructure value destruction collective margin compression and disciplined selling through Monte Carlo valuations.

## Executive Summary
In this comprehensive interview, NYU Professor Aswath Damodaran—the "Dean of Valuation"—shares his investment philosophy and analysis of the AI boom, revealing how major technological shifts inevitably create bubbles through human over-optimism while ultimately destroying value for infrastructure builders. His core thesis: AI will lower profit margins collectively across companies despite individual winners, because when everyone adopts the same productivity tools, nobody gains competitive advantage—similar to SAT prep spending that weaponizes the process without improving collective outcomes. The interview reveals his 30-45 stock portfolio approach (low turnover of 3-4 stocks annually), Monte Carlo simulation framework for buy/sell triggers, and increasing allocation to uncorrelated assets as market valuations stretch.

Damodaran predicts one or two AI infrastructure investors will see huge payoffs in a winner-take-all dynamic, but collectively the tens of billions in AI capital expenditure will generate negative net present value. He uses historical infrastructure investment patterns (railroads 1860s, telecom 1990s) to illustrate how builders of the future—AT&T laying phone lines, Cisco in the dot-com era—rarely capture value despite creating enormous societal benefits. Instead, later adopters like Netflix and Meta enjoy subsidized infrastructure costs from excess capacity. Current AI infrastructure companies (Nvidia, Microsoft, Meta) face this same dynamic, with the additional risk of cross-ownership entanglement creating systemic vulnerabilities similar to Asian family group structures.

On portfolio management, Damodaran emphasizes buying at the right price trumps buying great companies or superior management, using his 2018 Nvidia purchase and 2019 Tesla buy after the "$420 funding secured" collapse as examples of patience rewarded. His sell discipline uses Monte Carlo distributions to automate triggers—buying at 30th percentile (20% margin of safety) and selling at 70th percentile to remove emotional attachment to winning positions. He maintains watch lists of companies like Mercado Libre and Palantir waiting for price corrections, revisiting valuations continuously rather than using stale original assessments.

Critical insights include: Tim Cook's restraint avoiding large AI acquisitions or crazy investments demonstrates top-tier management by acting Apple's age as mature cash machine; companies aging faster in 21st century (25-30 year cycles vs 70-80 year cycles in 20th century); DeepSeek's efficient AI development threatens infrastructure buildout narrative that you need extraordinarily expensive chips and huge data centers; correlation across asset classes now nearly one making diversification harder (real estate, international equities, Bitcoin all move with stocks); and the danger of getting out of markets correctly but never getting back in (2008 crisis refugees still in cash 17 years later).

## Notable Quotes

> "Investing is about buying something at the right price. It's not about buying great companies. It's not about buying superior management. It's about buying at the right price. And that animates almost every one of my choices."

> "My prediction overall, I think AI is going to lower profit margins collectively across companies."

> "There will be one or two players who make those investments. So, the investment is going to pay off big time. They're going to it's going to be a winner take all business. Collectively though, if you take all of these companies that are spending tens of billions of dollars, they're going to step back and that collective investment is going to have a negative net present value."

> "I'm wary about where we are at the market in terms of pricing, in terms of what we're building in. It seems while there's a path where we can justify where we are today, there are also multiple pathways where things can go bad."

> "I don't have enough confidence no matter how much work I put into an investment to put my money in five stocks or four stocks because I think too much rides on it. So I spread my bets."

> "If you make this about I need to know what a product is, you're going to end up in the Buffett trap, which is you're going to own a lot of Coca-Cola and read a lot of the Washington Post and the older you get, the more disconnected you're going to get from the market."

> "I would love to have a movie made about somebody who made their company smaller because they'd reached a peak because that to me is a sign that you're really managing a company the way it should be managed."

> "Tim Cook should be put in the list of top managers of all time. The fact that he hasn't done a large acquisition or gone crazy on AI investment to me is a good sign. It's a sign that he says, 'Look, we're a mature company.'"

> "If you're a middle-aged company that keeps trying to squeeze yourself into tight jeans because that's what you used to wear when you were a teenager, I don't want to have anything to do with you."

> "I think any major change in the way we live and work is going to create a bubble. Why? Because that's what human beings do. They overreach. They're over optimistic."

> "Collectively think of how much money we spend on SAT prep now and then ask yourself, are we collectively better off? The answer is no. If everybody's spending on it, they're all getting the same benefit. You have to spend to keep up with everybody else but you're not getting the benefit."

> "The reason they were able to invest so much in infrastructure and borrow money to do it was they were building into a monopoly. With AI you're putting this on steroids you're building infrastructure into what's definitely not going to be a monopoly."

> "If one of them makes a big enough mistake and you start some of this play out, you can make a big enough mistake as a single entity to start dragging other players into the game."

> "DeepSeek brought in those few weeks that it roiled markets is that can't happen that all of this money that AI is spending building an infrastructure could be blown up by an outside technology that delivers much of what AI is promising with a fraction of the investment."

> "Most of these books don't have a chapter on when you should sell, right? We focus so much on buying the right things that we don't talk enough about when do those right things need to get sold."

> "Sometimes you got to automate the process saying look you know I'm going to buy it but I'm also going to put in a limit sell if it hits this price within the next 6 months."

> "There's still people who got out of market after the 2008 crisis that have not gone back into stocks."

> "Your time horizon is not your life. It's what your investment is designed to do, right? The people that you're managing the portfolio will change over time."

> "I find that writing allows me to think through my confusion and often when I think through my confusion, writing about that confusion actually helps as well."
